Beyond Observable Behavior

Beyond Observable Behavior: Measuring and Modifying the Function of Thought in School-Based Behavioral Assessment and Intervention

Rob Spain, BCBA, IBA — Kings Canyon Unified School District; Behavior School Cristal Lopez, BCBA — Kings Canyon Unified School District

With contributions from: Megan Caluza, BCBA — Berkeley Unified School District

Presented at the California Association for Behavior Analysis (CalABA) Annual Conference, Sacramento, California, March 7, 2026.

Abstract

Students with severe externalizing behavior disorders frequently fail to respond to consequence-based behavior intervention plans (BIPs) despite technically sound functional behavior assessments (FBAs). This paper argues that traditional behavioral assessment, while highly effective for externally mediated behavior, contains a systematic gap: it does not account for internally mediated behavior driven by rigid verbal relations and psychological inflexibility. Drawing on Relational Frame Theory (Hayes, Barnes-Holmes, & Roche, 2001), Relational Density Theory (Belisle & Dixon, 2020), and the Acceptance and Commitment Training (ACT) model (Dixon, Paliliunas, & Belisle, 2019), we describe the AIM (Accept and Innovate Mindfully) framework — a four-phase ACT-informed FBA and BIP model implemented across K-12 settings in two California school districts. In a quasi-experimental longitudinal case series (N = 4; grades 5 through 12), participants demonstrated a 58.3% mean increase in psychological flexibility on the Child Psychological Flexibility Questionnaire (CPFQ) and a 75% mean reduction in targeted maladaptive behaviors across an 8-month intervention period. Reliable Change Index (RCI) criteria (> 50% CPFQ composite gain) were met by 100% of participants at a median of 5 months, with gains significant at the 95% confidence level (z > 1.96). These findings suggest the AIM ACT model is a viable, generalizable framework for diverse K-12 educational settings and support the integration of ACT-based assessment and intervention into school-based behavior support systems. Implications for the scope of practice of Board Certified Behavior Analysts are discussed.

Keywords: functional behavior assessment, acceptance and commitment training, AIM curriculum, psychological flexibility, CPFQ, school-based intervention, relational frame theory, behavior intervention plan

Introduction

The functional behavior assessment has long served as the cornerstone of individualized behavior support in school settings (O’Neill et al., 1997; Umbreit, Ferro, Liaupsin, & Lane, 2007). By identifying the environmental antecedents and consequences that maintain problem behavior, FBAs enable practitioners to develop function-based BIPs that teach replacement behaviors, modify setting events, and rearrange contingencies. This model has demonstrated strong empirical support, particularly for behaviors maintained by social positive reinforcement (attention), social negative reinforcement (escape from task demands), or access to tangible items (Hanley, Iwata, & McCord, 2003).

However, a growing body of behavior-analytic literature suggests that this model encounters consistent limitations when applied to students whose behavior is primarily mediated by private verbal events — specifically, rigid rules, evaluative self-narratives, and experiential avoidance (Dixon, Hayes, & Belisle, 2023; Törneke, Luciano, & Valdivia Salas, 2008). For these students, the observable antecedent captured in ABC data is not the functional antecedent. Rather, the functional antecedent is the thought triggered by the environment: an automatically derived verbal relation such as “if I am redirected, then I am stupid” or “if I attempt the task, then I will fail.” Standard consequence manipulation does not disrupt these internal contingencies, and BIPs targeting only the external environment frequently fail to produce durable behavior change (Dixon et al., 2018).

This paper presents an integrated assessment and intervention framework — implemented across two California school districts — that extends the standard FBA process to assess and directly target the function of thought. The framework draws on three interconnected theoretical models: Relational Frame Theory (RFT; Hayes et al., 2001), which provides the basic science of language and cognition underlying the approach; Relational Density Theory (RDT; Belisle & Dixon, 2020), which accounts for the resistance to change exhibited by highly fused verbal networks; and Acceptance and Commitment Training (ACT; Dixon et al., 2019), which provides the clinical technology for modifying psychological flexibility in school-relevant contexts.

Part 1: The Assessment Phase — Application of Functional Analysis to the Function of Thought

Rob Spain, BCBA, IBA

Background: The Gap in Traditional FBA

Standard FBA methodology is organized around the three-term contingency: antecedent → behavior → consequence (Cooper, Heron, & Heward, 2020). Indirect assessments, structured interviews, and systematic direct observation are used to identify reliable environmental correlates of problem behavior, from which a hypothesized function is derived. This model has excellent construct validity for the detection and modification of directly contingency-shaped behavior.

The critical limitation emerges when behavior is rule-governed. As students acquire complex verbal repertoires through the arbitrarily applicable relational responding described by RFT, their behavior increasingly comes under the control of derived stimulus relations — verbal rules that have never been directly trained but are derived through combinatorial entailment (Hayes et al., 2001). A student who has formed the relational network “teacher = authority = rejection = I am unlovable” will respond to routine teacher redirection with disproportionate emotional and behavioral escalation that is poorly predicted by the immediate environmental antecedent alone.

Relational Density Theory formalizes this problem. Belisle and Dixon (2020) propose that verbal networks gain “density” as more nodes and bidirectional frames are added through a history of derived relational responding. Dense networks exhibit high coherence — the behavior-governing properties of the network become resistant to disconfirmation by direct contingency manipulation — analogous to the resistance to extinction observed in conditioned reinforcers after extensive training histories. When a student’s self-narrative is sufficiently dense, simply rearranging consequences will not alter the network’s functional control over behavior (Dixon, Hayes, & Belisle, 2023).

The KCUSD Assessment Model: A Tiered Framework

Kings Canyon Unified School District developed a five-tier behavior support system that stratifies assessment intensity by case complexity. Tiers 1 through 3 represent universal supports, targeted group interventions, and individualized FBA/BIP processes, respectively. Tier 4 represents the primary context for the ACT-informed assessment model described here: cases involving persistent externalizing behavior that has failed to respond to one or more function-based BIPs, where clinical judgment and latency data suggest internal mediation.

Tier 4 assessment proceeds along one of two pathways based on functional indicators. Pathway A, modeled on Hanley, Jin, Vanselow, and Hanratty (2014), applies Practical Functional Assessment (PFA) and Skill-Based Treatment (SBT) for behaviors maintained by external contingencies and skill deficits. Pathway B — the subject of this paper — applies the ACT-informed battery for behaviors where latency data, verbal report, and precursor topography suggest psychological inflexibility as the functional mechanism.

The Pathway B decision rule employs three primary indicators: (a) a latency from antecedent delivery to behavioral onset exceeding 30 seconds, (b) observable precursor behaviors during the latency period consistent with internal verbal activity (e.g., mumbling, self-talking, gaze avoidance, emotional escalation without clear external trigger), and (c) student verbal report of interfering thoughts or feelings during or after incidents.

Phase 1: Open-Ended Interview and ACT Matrix

The assessment battery begins with a structured open-ended interview designed to elicit the student’s private event repertoire. Questions are adapted from ACT-based assessment protocols (Luoma, Hayes, & Walser, 2017) and focus on identifying (a) the verbal content of interfering thoughts and feelings, (b) the behavioral strategies the student uses to reduce or avoid those private events (experiential avoidance), and (c) what the student cares about and what kind of person they want to be (values identification).

Interview data are immediately organized into an ACT Matrix (Polk & Schoendorff, 2014), a two-dimensional mapping tool with axes representing inner/outer experience (vertical) and toward/away movement relative to values (horizontal). The matrix operationalizes the six ACT processes in a single visual tool, revealing the functional relationship between private events (inner-away: fused thoughts and difficult feelings), avoidance behavior (outer-away: the problem behaviors that reduce contact with those events), personal values (inner-toward: what matters), and values-consistent behavior (outer-toward: what the student wants to do). In assessment, the matrix serves as a functional hypothesis — a visual representation of the private event contingency maintaining the problem behavior.

Phase 2: Direct Observation with Latency Recording

Standard ABC observation is modified to include systematic latency recording — the interval from the onset of the antecedent stimulus to the onset of the target behavior or its most proximal precursor. This modification captures empirical evidence for the mediated delay in responding that characterizes internally driven behavior. All observable precursor behaviors during the latency window are recorded verbatim, providing direct observational data on the student’s internal processing in naturalistic settings.

This procedure is theoretically grounded in the distinction between the nominal antecedent (the observable environmental event) and the functional antecedent (the privately derived verbal relation triggered by that event; cf. Tarbox, Dixon, Sturmey, & Matson, 2014). Extended latencies with precursor activity suggest that the observable antecedent functions as a discriminative stimulus for a sequence of derived verbal responding, and that the problem behavior is evoked by that internal event — not directly by the environmental antecedent.

Phase 3: Latency-Based Functional Analysis (Fusion Hierarchy Assessment)

The experimental component of the assessment uses a latency-based functional analysis format to construct a fusion hierarchy — a rank ordering of verbal statements by their function-governing strength. Drawing from the literature on experimental functional analysis adapted for school settings (Hanley et al., 2003), brief paired conditions are run for each verbal statement identified in Phase 1:

  • Validating condition: The examiner expresses agreement with or alignment with the statement (e.g., “I think you’re right that this is really hard for you.”)
  • Challenging condition: The examiner expresses disagreement or contradiction of the statement (e.g., “I actually think you can do this. Let’s try.”)

Differential latency to precursor onset across conditions provides an index of the statement’s functional role in governing behavior. Statements that produce reliably shorter latencies in the validating condition and longer latencies in the challenging condition are hypothesized to be functionally controlling — the student’s behavior is being governed by the literal truth of the verbal rule. Those showing no differential latency are hypothesized to be non-functional. The resulting hierarchy informs prioritization of defusion targets in the BIP.

Phase 4: CPFQ Administration and Values Assessment

The Comprehensive Psychological Flexibility Questionnaire (CPFQ; Dixon et al., 2019) is administered in two parallel forms: student self-report (age 8 and older) and caregiver/teacher report. The instrument yields a total psychological flexibility score and subscale scores across the six ACT processes: acceptance, cognitive defusion, present-moment awareness, self-as-context, values clarity, and committed action. Total scores below 2.0 on the 0–4 scale indicate significant psychological rigidity in one or more processes and are used to guide the prioritization of ACT targets in the BIP.

Values assessment is conducted concurrently, using an age-adapted values card sort, a structured values list, or a preference-assessment format for younger students. Identifying two to five personally meaningful values is essential to the intervention phase, as values provide the motivative augmentals that support behavioral activation in the presence of psychological discomfort. Consistent with Hayes, Strosahl, and Wilson (2012), values are operationalized in behavioral terms: not as abstract attributes but as quality-of-action descriptors that can be directly trained and reinforced.

Part 2: The Intervention Phase — ACT-Informed BIPs and Values-Based Programming

Cristal Lopez, BCBA

Theoretical Grounding: The Intersection of SBT and ACT

The behavior intervention plan developed from this assessment integrates two empirically supported behavioral frameworks: Hanley’s Skill-Based Treatment (SBT) model (Hanley et al., 2014), which targets the external behavioral skill deficits maintaining problem behavior through functional communication training, tolerance building, and cooperation training; and Acceptance and Commitment Training, which targets the internal verbal processes maintaining experiential avoidance and psychological rigidity (Dixon et al., 2019).

These frameworks are theoretically complementary rather than competing. SBT operates primarily at the level of directly contingency-shaped behavior — it teaches students to request preferred items, tolerate denial of access, and cooperate with demands through the systematic manipulation of external contingencies and explicit skill instruction. ACT operates at the level of rule-governed behavior — it teaches students to contact private events without avoidance, hold verbal content as contextually derived rather than literal truth, identify what matters, and engage in committed action toward values even in the presence of discomfort.

The integration rests on a key clinical distinction: toleration without emotional regulation is compliance, not flexibility. A student trained to comply with demands through external contingency management who remains rigidly fused to a negative self-narrative has not developed durable psychological flexibility. The ACT component of the BIP ensures that behavioral compliance is anchored in the student’s own values repertoire and that the student develops the internal resources to maintain behavioral gains across settings, staff, and time.

Constructing the ACT-Informed BIP

The ACT-informed BIP is organized around five components, each mapped to assessment data:

1. Antecedent Modifications (Acceptance-Based). Antecedent strategies are designed not to eliminate contact with aversive stimuli — which would reinforce experiential avoidance — but to reduce the behavioral control of those stimuli through acceptance-based framing. Staff training includes instruction in “validating without agreeing” — acknowledging the student’s internal experience while not reinforcing fusion or avoidance: “That sounds really hard. And I still need you to try.”

2. Defusion Procedures. Defusion targets are selected from the fusion hierarchy, prioritizing statements with the highest functional control. Defusion is implemented through age-appropriate metaphors and exercises (Greco & Hayes, 2008), adapted for developmental level. For early elementary students (Grades 1–3), concrete, sensory-based metaphors such as “thoughts are like weather” or “your brain is having a thought” are used. For adolescents, more abstract defusion techniques including the “Thought Train” exercise and the “Hands as Thoughts” metaphor are incorporated. All defusion procedures are operationalized as behavioral teaching targets with measurable responses and explicit prompt hierarchies.

3. Values-Aligned Replacement Behaviors. Rather than identifying replacement behaviors solely on the basis of communicative function (as in standard FCT), ACT-informed BIPs anchor replacement behaviors in the student’s identified values. A student who identifies “respect” as a core value may be taught to ask for a break using the frame: “Asking for a break is something respectful students do. It shows you can manage hard feelings.” This values-linkage functions as a conditioned motivating operation, increasing the relative reinforcing value of the replacement behavior relative to the avoidance behavior.

4. Acceptance and Present-Moment Skills. For students with high experiential avoidance scores on the CPFQ, structured acceptance exercises are incorporated as proactive skill-building activities in low-demand contexts. These are implemented using the AIM (Acceptance and Integration Meeting) curriculum framework (Dixon et al., 2019) and materials from Thriving Adolescent (Hayes & Ciarrochi, 2015), adapted for the school context. AIM sessions are conducted weekly in a group or individual format and explicitly target the CPFQ subscales identified as areas of weakness in the baseline assessment.

5. Progress Monitoring via CPFQ. The CPFQ is re-administered at scheduled intervals (minimally at 6 and 12 weeks following BIP implementation) to provide a quantitative index of psychological flexibility change concurrent with behavioral data. This dual-data system — behavioral frequency/intensity alongside CPFQ scores — allows practitioners to evaluate whether behavioral change is accompanied by the internal flexibility gains hypothesized to produce durable outcomes.

Part 3: Implementation and Fidelity — Scaling ACT-Informed Practices Across School Sites

Rob Spain, BCBA, IBA

Challenges of Implementation in Public School Settings

The adoption of ACT-informed assessment and intervention practices in public school settings faces a set of implementation challenges distinct from those encountered in clinical or residential settings. School practitioners operate under substantial time constraints, with FBA processes often compressed to comply with IEP timelines. The ACT battery described here requires dedicated assessment time — minimally four to six sessions across two to three weeks — that exceeds the time typically allocated for Tier 3 FBA processes.

Staff training presents an additional challenge. The effective implementation of defusion procedures, values-based programming, and acceptance-oriented antecedent strategies requires practitioner competencies that are not currently emphasized in BCBA training programs. Paraeducators and classroom teachers — who deliver the majority of behavioral intervention — require explicit training in ACT-consistent language and response patterns, which differ significantly from the contingency-management language that dominates existing behavior support training.

Berkeley Unified School District’s implementation model addressed these challenges through a collaborative consultation structure, embedding school psychologists, special education teachers, and paraeducators as co-implementers in the assessment and intervention process. This model distributed implementation burden, increased ecological validity, and provided natural opportunities for in-vivo coaching of ACT-consistent staff behavior during BIP implementation.

Fidelity Measurement

Intervention fidelity was assessed across three domains: (a) procedural fidelity of defusion and acceptance exercises, measured via behavioral skills training (BST) checklists completed by supervising BCBAs during direct observation; (b) fidelity of staff language in daily interactions, assessed via momentary time sampling of ACT-consistent versus ACT-inconsistent verbal responses during the intervention period; and (c) fidelity of CPFQ administration and scoring, ensured through standardized protocol sheets and inter-rater reliability checks between BCBA and teacher raters.

Staff training proceeded through a modified BST sequence: written instruction (ACT intervention manual), modeling (BCBA demonstration with student), rehearsal (staff practice with simulated scenarios), and feedback (in-vivo coaching with written and verbal feedback). Criterion for independent implementation was set at 80% procedural fidelity across three consecutive observation probes.

Social Validity

Social validity data were collected from students, caregivers, and school staff using adapted versions of the Goal Attainment Scale (Kiresuk & Sherman, 1968) and a purpose-built ACT Intervention Social Validity Survey. Consistently high ratings across domains of goal relevance, acceptability of procedures, and perceived effectiveness were obtained, with several student participants independently endorsing the intervention as “helping me understand myself better” — a response pattern consistent with the theorized mechanism of action (increased psychological flexibility rather than mere compliance).

Participants

Four students receiving special education services in California public school settings were enrolled in the AIM ACT intervention and contributed data to the present case series. Participants were identified for Tier 4 services after failing to respond to at least one prior function-based BIP. All participants met the Pathway B decision criteria described in Part 1 (latency > 30 seconds, observable precursor behavior, self-reported interfering private events). Design and Procedure

The present study employed a quasi-experimental longitudinal case series design. This design is appropriate for initial effectiveness investigations in applied settings where randomization and withholding treatment are ethically and logistically impractical (Kazdin, 2011; Morgan & Morgan, 2009). Each participant received the full AIM ACT intervention as described in Parts 1 through 3 of this paper. No comparison or control condition was implemented.

Intervention. The AIM (Accept and Innovate Mindfully) curriculum was delivered at an average dosage of two 30-minute sessions per week across the 8-month study period. Sessions incorporated the six ACT core processes operationalized for school settings: acceptance, cognitive defusion, present-moment awareness, self-as-context, values clarification, and committed action. BIPs were modified concurrently to incorporate ACT-consistent antecedent strategies, defusion procedures, and values-linked replacement behaviors as described in Part 2.

Data Collection. Data were triangulated from two independent sources: (a) Daily Behavior Point Sheets, embedded in each participant’s existing BIP monitoring system, which tracked frequency counts of targeted “away” behaviors (disruption, aggression, elopement/escape) across all school settings; and (b) the Child Psychological Flexibility Questionnaire (CPFQ; Dixon et al., 2019), administered in parallel student self-report and caregiver/teacher forms at baseline and at bimonthly intervals (Months 2, 4, 5, and 8). CPFQ scores are reported as percentage change from baseline composite score.

At baseline, participants showed severe psychological inflexibility consistent with clinical referral criteria (CPFQ composite < 2.0/4.0). By Month 2, a mean 20% improvement was observed (SD = 5.82), consistent with initial skill acquisition across defusion and acceptance domains. The RCI threshold of 50% composite gain — selected a priori as the criterion for clinically meaningful change (Jacobson & Truax, 1991) — was approached by Month 4 (M = 46.5%) and exceeded at Month 5 (M = 50.8%, SD = 6.45; p < .05). Gains continued through the 8-month measurement period, reaching a mean of 58.3% above baseline (SD = 5.20), indicating maintenance and continued growth rather than a plateau or regression effect.

Reductions across all three behavior categories were clinically and practically significant. Aggression produced the largest proportional reduction (83.8%), followed closely by disruption (80.3%). Escape/elopement, which is theoretically most resistant to purely internal-mechanism interventions given its potential for direct external negative reinforcement, produced a 50.0% reduction — still meeting standard clinical benchmarks for meaningful intervention response. Aggregate across behavior categories, participants demonstrated a 75.0% mean reduction in daily targeted behavior episodes, from a baseline mean of 8.8 episodes per day to 2.2 at Month 8.

Statistical Validity: Reliable Change Index

The Reliable Change Index (Jacobson & Truax, 1991) was applied to CPFQ composite data to determine whether observed changes exceeded the threshold attributable to measurement error. The RCI threshold was set at > 50% change in CPFQ composite score. All four participants (n = 4; 100%) met or exceeded this threshold by Month 6. The observed growth trajectory was significant at the 95% confidence level (z > 1.96), indicating that the observed flexibility gains represent genuine individual change rather than regression to the mean or instrument variability.

The convergence of statistically validated CPFQ gains with behaviorally measured reductions in problem behavior supports the theoretical model: the mechanism of change is the modification of internal psychological flexibility, and behavioral improvement follows as a downstream consequence of that internal change, rather than being produced directly by contingency manipulation.

Discussion

The AIM ACT longitudinal case series provides initial empirical support for the four-phase framework described across the preceding sections of this paper. Across four participants spanning elementary through high school — representing therapeutic day, self-contained, general education, and resource room settings — the model produced clinically meaningful and statistically reliable improvements in both psychological flexibility and targeted problem behavior across an 8-month period.

The cross-developmental scope of the sample warrants particular emphasis. A persistent critique of ACT-based interventions in pediatric populations has been the assumption that the verbal repertoires required for defusion, acceptance, and values work are developmentally inaccessible to younger or cognitively lower-functioning students (see Greco & Hayes, 2008, for a review). The inclusion of a 5th-grade participant (Jeronimo) in the validated sample directly addresses this concern. The observation that developmentally adapted ACT procedures produced RCI-significant CPFQ gains in an elementary-age student — alongside high school participants — suggests that the limiting variable is not developmental age per se, but the functional availability of the relevant verbal repertoires, which can be assessed and built at multiple developmental levels through appropriate instructional design.

The behavior reduction data further suggest that the model produces practically significant outcomes in the behavior categories most disruptive to school placement stability: aggression (83.8% reduction) and disruption (80.3% reduction). The more modest escape/elopement reduction (50.0%), while still clinically meaningful, is consistent with the theoretical prediction that escape-maintained behavior has the most robust external reinforcement history and therefore the greatest resistance to intervention approaches that do not simultaneously address the direct contingency. Future iterations of the AIM model may benefit from more intensive SBT integration specifically for escape-maintained topographies.

Limitations

Several methodological limitations constrain the conclusions that may be drawn from the present data. First, the quasi-experimental case series design does not permit causal attribution; the observed improvements may reflect maturation, concurrent treatment, or regression to the mean, though the RCI analysis substantially mitigates the last of these concerns. Second, the sample size (N = 4) limits statistical power and generalizability; replication with larger samples and across diverse geographic and demographic contexts is required before broad clinical recommendations are warranted. Third, the fusion hierarchy assessment protocol used in Phase 1 has not yet undergone standardized reliability and validity testing; inter-rater reliability data should be reported in future research. Fourth, the CPFQ, while showing promising psychometric properties in the published literature (Dixon et al., 2019), requires further validation specifically for school-aged populations in IDEA-regulated special education contexts. Future research should address these limitations through adequately powered quasi-experimental or randomized controlled designs with appropriate experimental controls and formal inter-rater reliability protocols.

Conclusions

This paper has described a systematic extension of the functional behavior assessment process to address behavior mediated by private verbal events — specifically, rigid verbal networks characterized by cognitive fusion, experiential avoidance, and values disconnection. The four-phase assessment battery (open-ended interview + ACT Matrix, latency-based direct observation, fusion hierarchy functional analysis, and CPFQ administration with values assessment) provides a coherent and operationalizable method for identifying the function of thought. The ACT-informed BIP that follows from this assessment directly targets the internal contingencies maintaining the behavior, supplementing rather than replacing the skill-based and contingency-based components of the standard BIP.

Outcome data from two California school districts indicate clinically meaningful behavioral improvements concurrent with increases in measured psychological flexibility — a convergence consistent with the theoretical model and with the broader ACT outcome literature (A-Tjak et al., 2015). These results support the integration of ACT-based assessment and intervention practices into school-based behavior support systems, particularly for Tier 4 cases involving severe externalizing behavior that has not responded to standard FBA/BIP methodology.

The practical implication is straightforward: for students whose behavior is driven by rigid internal verbal rules, the function that matters is not the function of the external consequence — it is the function of the thought.
